About Robotics

Understanding the science of designing, building, and operating intelligent machines.

What is Robotics?

Robotics is the interdisciplinary field combining engineering and computer science to design, build, and operate machines that sense, think, and act in the physical world.

History of Robotics

From the first industrial arm in 1961 to today's autonomous humanoids, robotics has evolved through decades of breakthroughs in mechanics, electronics, and AI.

How Robots Work

Robots perceive their environment through sensors, process information with onboard logic or AI, and act on the world through actuators and motors.

Main Components

Every robot relies on a core set of parts: sensors, controllers, actuators, a power source, an end effector, and software that ties it all together.

Types of Robots

From factory floors to deep space, robots take many forms built for specific missions.

Industrial Robots

Fixed robotic arms automating manufacturing, welding, and assembly lines.

Advantage: Precision and 24/7 throughput.

Example: FANUC, KUKA arms.

Service Robots

Robots assisting with everyday tasks in homes and businesses.

Advantage: Convenience and labor support.

Example: Robotic vacuum cleaners.

Medical Robots

Precision systems assisting surgeons and supporting patient care.

Advantage: Sub-millimeter accuracy.

Example: Surgical assistance systems.

Educational Robots

Programmable kits that teach coding, electronics, and STEM concepts.

Advantage: Hands-on learning.

Example: Classroom robotics kits.

Military Robots

Unmanned systems for reconnaissance, logistics, and bomb disposal.

Advantage: Removes humans from danger.

Example: EOD disposal robots.

Agricultural Robots

Autonomous machines for planting, harvesting, and crop monitoring.

Advantage: Higher yield, less labor.

Example: Autonomous harvesters.

Space Exploration Robots

Rovers and probes exploring planets and gathering scientific data.

Advantage: Survives extreme environments.

Example: Mars rovers.

Humanoid Robots

Bipedal robots designed to mimic human movement and interaction.

Advantage: Operates in human-built spaces.

Example: Research humanoid platforms.

Autonomous Vehicles

Self-driving cars and shuttles that navigate roads without a driver.

Advantage: Improved road safety potential.

Example: Self-driving test fleets.

Underwater Robots

ROVs and AUVs exploring oceans, pipelines, and shipwrecks.

Advantage: Reaches extreme depths.

Example: Deep-sea survey ROVs.

Drone Robots

Aerial robots for surveying, delivery, photography, and inspection.

Advantage: Fast aerial access.

Example: Quadcopter drones.

Rescue Robots

Robots that search disaster sites for survivors in hazardous terrain.

Advantage: Operates in collapsed structures.

Example: Search-and-rescue crawlers.
